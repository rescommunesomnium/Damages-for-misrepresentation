Misrepresentation — Damages

Fraudulent & innocent misrepresentation · Misrepresentation Act 1967 · Smith New Court Securities Ltd v Scrimgeour Vickers [1997]

Comparing damages for fraudulent and innocent misrepresentation, including the higher amount available where the rule in Smith New Court applies.

Fraudulent misrepresentation
Deceit / s.2(1) Misrepresentation Act 1967
Basis
Tort of deceit. Where s.2(1) applies (negligent misrep), the same measure is used by statutory fiction — Royscot Trust v Rogerson [1991].
Measure of damages
All actual loss flowing from the transaction. The aim is to restore C to their pre-transaction position, not to what their position would have been under the contract (Doyle v Olby (Ironmongers) Ltd [1969]).
Remoteness test
Directness only — not reasonable foreseeability. All direct consequences are recoverable even if unforeseeable. This is far wider than the contract rule.
Pregnant-with-the-loss rule
Applies. The transaction itself is the damage-generating event. Subsequent falls in value — even if caused by an entirely separate, unforeseeable event — are recoverable so long as C was locked in from contract date (Smith New Court).
Contributory negligence
Not available against the tort of deceit: Standard Chartered Bank v Pakistan National Shipping Corp (No 2) [2002]. Available under s.2(1) on some authorities.
Consequential losses
Recoverable — e.g. loss of profits, wasted expenditure on the asset, costs of financing.
Innocent misrepresentation
s.2(2) Misrepresentation Act 1967
Basis
Statute only. The court has a discretion to award damages in lieu of rescission where it would be equitable to do so. Primary remedy is rescission.
Measure of damages
Generally the overvaluation at contract date — the difference between the price paid and the true value of what was received (William Sindall plc v Cambridgeshire CC [1994]). Contractual flavour.
Remoteness test
Closer to reasonable contemplation at the time of contracting — no direct analogy to deceit's wider rule.
Pregnant-with-the-loss rule
Does not apply. Loss is assessed at contract date. Subsequent market falls caused by unforeseeable events are not recoverable — the overvaluation figure is the ceiling.
Contributory negligence
Available. The court may reduce damages to reflect C's own fault in entering the transaction.
Consequential losses
Generally not recoverable — s.2(2) is a substitute for rescission, not a comprehensive damages regime.

Note: Royscot is controversial. Some academics and judges argue the "as if fraudulent" treatment of s.2(1) was a misreading of the legislative intent, but it remains binding Court of Appeal authority. For a detailed critical account drawing together the critiques of Treitel, Atiyah, and others, see J Devenney, 'Re-Examining Damages for Fraudulent Misrepresentation' in Commercial Contract Law (CUP 2012) (On MyStudies).

Smith New Court Securities Ltd v Scrimgeour Vickers [1997] AC 254

House of Lords · Lord Browne-Wilkinson

The leading authority on the measure of damages in deceit and the foundational source of the pregnant-with-the-loss principle. This speech is worth reading in full.

The facts

Smith New Court (SNC) were fraudulently induced by Scrimgeour's agent to purchase 28.41 million Ferranti shares at 82.25p per share — well above the market-making price of 78p. The fraudster falsely claimed two other bidders were competing to inflate the price. Shortly after purchase, and before SNC could sell, a completely separate and entirely unknown fraud at Ferranti itself came to light (this involved Ferranti - which operated in the defense sector - acquiring a US company that had entirely false accounts and illegally sold arms to South Africa and Iraq, and US President Bill Clinton's pick for Defence Secretary was in on it. The Ferranti share price collapsed. SNC eventually sold for around 30–44p per share.

"The transaction was a continuing one and the plaintiff was locked into an asset which was from the outset pregnant with the loss." — Lord Browne-Wilkinson, Smith New Court [1997] AC 254 at 267

The legal question

Could SNC recover not just the overvaluation at contract date (the difference between 82.25p and the true market value), but the full loss eventually suffered — including the loss caused by the entirely unforeseeable Ferranti fraud?

The House of Lords' reasoning

  • 1
    Entry into the transaction is the damage-generating act. The deceit caused SNC to enter the contract. That entry, not the subsequent Ferranti fraud, is what the law must undo.
  • 2
    Directness, not foreseeability. Remoteness in deceit is governed by directness (Doyle v Olby). The secondary collapse was a direct consequence of being locked into an asset bought at an inflated price.
  • 3
    No causation break. SNC could not have sold at or near true value at any point before the Ferranti fraud became public. The asset was always worth less than they paid. There was no "clean exit" window in which the chain could be severed.
  • 4
    Full loss recoverable. SNC were entitled to recover the price paid less the eventual proceeds, including all the loss caused by the secondary Ferranti fraud they had no knowledge of.

Rule applies when…

C paid above true value at contract date AND was locked in — unable to sell at or near true value — when a further event (foreseeable or not) depressed value further.

Rule does NOT apply when…

There was a window in which C could have sold at or near true value before the secondary event. A "break" in the chain severs the further losses. The fraudster is not liable for loss C could have avoided It follows that where there is a break in the chain, the measure of damages for a fall in asset value will be the same for fraudulent and innocent misrepresentation. The fraud measure is still more generous for other consequential losses (financing costs, expenditure on the asset), and in preventing the defendant from claiming contributory negligence. .

Contrast with innocent misrepresentation
Under s.2(2), damages are assessed at contract date. The subsequent Ferranti-type collapse would not be recoverable. The s.2(2) measure is essentially the overvaluation — what C overpaid — and nothing more. This is one of the most practically significant differences between the two regimes.
The Royscot extension
Because s.2(1) of the 1967 Act is treated as equivalent to fraud for damages purposes (Royscot Trust v Rogerson), the Smith New Court rule applies to negligent misrepresentation under s.2(1) too — even though there is no actual dishonesty. This extension remains academically contested. For a detailed critical account drawing together the critiques of Treitel, Atiyah, and others, see J Devenney, 'Re-Examining Damages for Fraudulent Misrepresentation' in Commercial Contract Law (CUP 2012) (On MyStudies).

Worked example — the Hartley shares

A constructed problem illustrating both regimes and the Smith New Court principle. Work through the timeline, then check the calculations.

The facts

In January 2022, Petra purchases 500,000 shares in Hartley plc from Marcus for £2.00 per share (total: £1,000,000). Marcus tells Petra that Hartley holds a confirmed government infrastructure contract worth £50m. This is false — Marcus knows the contract has not been awarded.

At the date of purchase, the true value of the shares (absent the false representation) is £1.40 per share.

In September 2022, entirely separately, Hartley's key supplier goes insolvent — an event completely unconnected to the misrepresentation and entirely unforeseeable. The share price collapses. Petra eventually sells in December 2022 at £0.30 per share.

Timeline

January 2022
The fraudulent misrepresentation and purchase
Petra pays £2.00/share. True value is £1.40/share. Overvaluation = 60p per share. The transaction is already "pregnant with the loss" — Petra paid £300,000 more than the shares were worth.
Jan–Sep 2022
Petra is locked in
Petra cannot sell at or near true value. The shares continue to trade around £1.40, but she still cannot recover her overpayment.
September 2022
Unforeseeable secondary event — supplier insolvency
Hartley's key supplier collapses. Entirely unconnected to Marcus's fraud. Unforeseeable. Share price falls sharply.
December 2022
Petra sells at £0.30/share
Total proceeds: £150,000. Total loss on the transaction: £850,000.

The calculations

Fraudulent misrepresentation / s.2(1)

Price paid£1,000,000
Less: disposal proceeds−£150,000
Total recovery£850,000

Includes the £500,000 attributable to the unforeseeable supplier insolvency. Recoverable because the transaction locked Petra in — Smith New Court applies. No foreseeability required.

Innocent misrepresentation / s.2(2)

Price paid£1,000,000
Less: true value at contract date−£700,000
Total recovery£300,000

Capped at the overvaluation at contract date. The £500,000 attributable to the supplier insolvency is not recoverable — Smith New Court does not apply. Loss assessed at January 2022 only.

The gap: £550,000. That is the additional exposure Marcus faces for actual fraud (or a s.2(1) claim) compared to a s.2(2) claim. The entire difference arises from the Smith New Court rule — Petra was locked into a transaction that was from the outset pregnant with the eventual loss.

Discussion questions

  1. Would the answer differ if Petra could have sold at £1.40 in March 2022 but chose not to? Consider the causation break point in Smith New Court.
  2. If Marcus had merely been careless (not dishonest), how does Royscot Trust v Rogerson affect the analysis? Do you think that result is defensible?
  3. Petra contributed to her loss by not investigating the contract claim. Does this affect anything? Compare the fraud and s.2(1) positions.
